## Mathematical Content

### 1. Setup and Notation

Let $\mathcal{A}$ be a unital *-algebra (or more generally an Archimedean order unit space $(V, V^+, e)$). The **state space** is:
$$S(\mathcal{A}) = \{\varphi : \mathcal{A} \to \mathbb{C} \mid \varphi \text{ positive, } \varphi(1) = 1\}$$
equipped with the weak-* topology. This is a compact convex set.

For a state $\varphi \in S(\mathcal{A})$, the **GNS construction** gives a triple $(\pi_\varphi, \mathcal{H}_\varphi, \xi_\varphi)$ where:
- $\pi_\varphi : \mathcal{A} \to B(\mathcal{H}_\varphi)$ is a *-representation
- $\xi_\varphi \in \mathcal{H}_\varphi$ is a cyclic unit vector
- $\varphi(a) = \langle \pi_\varphi(a)\xi_\varphi, \xi_\varphi \rangle$

The **commutant** of the GNS representation is $\pi_\varphi(\mathcal{A})'$.

### 2. The Face-Commutant Correspondence (Classical, Alfsen-Shultz)

**Theorem (Alfsen-Shultz).** Let $\varphi \in S(\mathcal{A})$. The face $F_{S(\mathcal{A})}(\varphi)$ generated by $\varphi$ in the state space is affinely isomorphic to the state space of the commutant restricted to the cyclic subspace:
$$F_{S(\mathcal{A})}(\varphi) \cong \{T \in \pi_\varphi(\mathcal{A})'^+ : \langle T\xi_\varphi, \xi_\varphi \rangle = 1\}$$

More precisely, the map $T \mapsto \omega_T$ where $\omega_T(a) = \langle \pi_\varphi(a) T \xi_\varphi, \xi_\varphi \rangle$ is an affine isomorphism.

**Consequence:**
$$\dim(F_{S(\mathcal{A})}(\varphi)) = \dim_{\mathbb{R}}(\pi_\varphi(\mathcal{A})'^{sa}) - 1$$

where $\pi_\varphi(\mathcal{A})'^{sa}$ denotes the real vector space of self-adjoint elements of the commutant.

**Key references:** 
- Alfsen, E.M., Shultz, F.W.: *State Spaces of Operator Algebras* (2001)
- Alfsen, E.M., Shultz, F.W.: *Geometry of State Spaces of Operator Algebras* (2003)
- The Radon-Nikodym type theorem for states: if $\psi \leq \lambda \varphi$ then $\psi(a) = \langle \pi_\varphi(a) T \xi_\varphi, \xi_\varphi \rangle$ for unique $T \in \pi_\varphi(\mathcal{A})'$ with $0 \leq T \leq \lambda I$.

### 3. The Abstract Face-Dimension Bound (Weis-Shirokov + Pataki)

**Theorem (Weis-Shirokov 2020, Thm 2).** Let $K$ be a convex set and $f : K \to \mathbb{R} \cup \{+\infty\}$ a generalized affine map. Let $C$ be a sublevel or level set of $f$ in $K$. For any $x \in C$:
$$\dim(F_K(x)) \leq \dim(F_C(x)) + 1$$

**Corollary (Weis-Shirokov 2020, Cor 5).** Under $\ell$ affine constraints:
$$\dim(F_K(x)) \leq \dim(F_C(x)) + \ell$$

**Pataki's Formula (2000, Thm 3.3.1, for cones).** For $F = K \cap \{Ax = b\}$ with $K$ a closed convex cone in finite dimensions:
$$\dim(\mathrm{face}(\bar{x}, F)) = \dim(\mathrm{face}(\bar{x}, K)) - m + \dim(\mathrm{face}(\bar{x}, K)^\perp \cap \mathcal{R}(A^*))$$

For extreme points (LHS = 0): $\dim(\mathrm{face}(\bar{x}, K)) \leq m$.

**Key reference:** 
- Weis, S., Shirokov, M.: *The face generated by a point, generalized affine constraints, and quantum theory*. J. Convex Anal. 28(3), 847-870 (2021). arXiv:2003.14302.
- Pataki, G.: *Geometry of Semidefinite Programming*. In: Handbook of SDP, Kluwer (2000).

### 4. Main Theorem: Barvinok-Pataki for State Spaces

**Theorem (Main Result).** Let $\mathcal{A}$ be a unital *-algebra and let $a_1, \ldots, a_m \in \mathcal{A}^{sa}$ be self-adjoint elements. Consider the affinely constrained state space:
$$C = \{\varphi \in S(\mathcal{A}) : \varphi(a_j) = \beta_j, \; j = 1, \ldots, m\}$$
(or inequality constraints $\varphi(a_j) \leq \beta_j$, or mixed). If $\varphi$ is an extreme point of $C$, then:
$$\dim_{\mathbb{R}}(\pi_\varphi(\mathcal{A})'^{sa}) \leq m + 1$$

Equivalently, if $\pi_\varphi \cong \bigoplus_{j=1}^k \sigma_j^{\oplus n_j}$ where $\sigma_j$ are pairwise inequivalent irreducible representations, then:
$$\sum_{j=1}^k n_j^2 \leq m + 1$$

**Proof sketch:**
1. By the face-commutant correspondence: $\dim(F_{S(\mathcal{A})}(\varphi)) = \dim_{\mathbb{R}}(\pi_\varphi(\mathcal{A})'^{sa}) - 1$.
2. By Weis-Shirokov (or by adapting Pataki's argument): if $\varphi$ is extreme in $C$ (i.e., $\dim(F_C(\varphi)) = 0$), then $\dim(F_{S(\mathcal{A})}(\varphi)) \leq m$.
3. Combining: $\dim_{\mathbb{R}}(\pi_\varphi(\mathcal{A})'^{sa}) - 1 \leq m$.
4. The commutant decomposition: $\pi_\varphi(\mathcal{A})' \cong \bigoplus_j M_{n_j}(\mathbb{C})$, so $\dim_{\mathbb{R}}(\pi_\varphi(\mathcal{A})'^{sa}) = \sum_j n_j^2$.

### 5. Recovery of Classical Cases

#### 5a. Abelian case → Carathéodory

If $\mathcal{A} = C(X)$ for a compact Hausdorff space $X$:
- $S(\mathcal{A})$ = probability measures on $X$
- All irreducible representations are 1-dimensional (point evaluations)
- $\pi_\varphi \cong \bigoplus_j \mathrm{ev}_{x_j}^{\oplus 1}$ means $\varphi$ is supported on finitely many points
- The bound $\sum_j 1^2 = k \leq m+1$ gives: extreme points under $m$ moment constraints are supported on at most $m+1$ points

This is precisely **Carathéodory's theorem** (or more precisely Richter-Rogosinski for moment problems, or Bauer's maximum principle + Choquet theory).

#### 5b. Matrix algebra case → Classical Barvinok-Pataki

If $\mathcal{A} = M_n(\mathbb{C})$:
- $S(\mathcal{A})$ = density matrices (states on $M_n$)
- There is only one irreducible representation (the identity), so $\pi_\varphi \cong \mathrm{id}^{\oplus r}$ where $r = \mathrm{rank}(\rho_\varphi)$
- The bound gives $r^2 \leq m+1$, i.e., $r(r+1)/2 \leq m$ after accounting for the trace constraint (if applicable; be careful with the counting here — the standard BP bound for $\{X \succeq 0 : \mathrm{Tr}(A_j X) = b_j\}$ gives $r(r+1)/2 \leq m$, which comes from $\dim(\mathrm{face}(X, S^n_+)) = r(r+1)/2$)

**IMPORTANT:** Be very careful about the exact relationship between the commutant dimension $r^2$ and the face dimension $r(r+1)/2$ of $S^n_+$. For $M_n(\mathbb{C})$:
- The face of $\rho$ in the PSD cone $S^n_+$ has dimension $r(r+1)/2$ (real symmetric matrices supported on $\mathrm{range}(\rho)$).
- But $S(\mathcal{A})$ is the state space (trace-1 positive matrices), so faces in $S(\mathcal{A})$ are faces of $S^n_+$ intersected with the trace hyperplane.
- The commutant $\pi_\varphi(M_n)' = \{T : [T, X] = 0 \;\forall X\}$. For the GNS of a rank-$r$ state, this is $M_r(\mathbb{C})$ acting on the $r$-dimensional cyclic subspace, giving $\dim_{\mathbb{R}}(M_r(\mathbb{C})^{sa}) = r^2$.
- So $r^2 \leq m+1$. The classical BP bound is $r(r+1)/2 \leq m$ (for the PSD cone, which is self-dual and the faces have the quadratic dimension formula).
- Reconcile: the standard BP works with the PSD *cone* (not the state space), and uses $\dim(\mathrm{face}(X, S^n_+)) = r(r+1)/2$. Our bound uses $\dim(\mathrm{face}(\varphi, S(\mathcal{A}))) = r^2 - 1$. These differ because $S^n_+$ faces have extra structure (they're *symmetric* matrix spaces, dimension $r(r+1)/2$), while the commutant is the *full* matrix algebra $M_r$, dimension $r^2$.

**This discrepancy needs careful treatment.** The resolution is:
- For the full matrix algebra $M_n(\mathbb{C})$, the face of a state $\rho$ of rank $r$ in $S(M_n)$ is indeed isomorphic to $S(M_r)$, which has dimension $r^2 - 1$ (density matrices of rank $\leq r$ form a set whose extreme points are rank-1, etc.). 
- The PSD cone face dimension $r(r+1)/2$ counts the real dimension of the face of $S^n_+$, which is $\{X \in S^n_+ : \mathrm{range}(X) \subseteq \mathrm{range}(\rho)\}$. This is the *real symmetric* matrices of that block, not Hermitian. For **complex** Hermitian matrices, the face dimension is $r^2$.
- For *real* SDP ($S^n_+ \subset S^n$ = real symmetric matrices), face dimension is $r(r+1)/2$, giving BP: $r(r+1)/2 \leq m$.
- For *complex* SDP ($\mathcal{H}^n_+ \subset \mathcal{H}^n$ = Hermitian matrices), face dimension is $r^2$, giving BP: $r^2 \leq m$.
- Our theorem naturally gives the complex version: $r^2 \leq m+1$. The "+1" comes from the trace normalisation (one constraint used to enforce $\varphi(1) = 1$).

**Make sure the paper handles real vs complex carefully throughout.** For real *-algebras (with trivial involution), you get the real version. State this cleanly.

#### 5c. Finite-dimensional C*-algebra case → Block-diagonal structure

If $\mathcal{A} = \bigoplus_{i=1}^N M_{d_i}(\mathbb{C})$:
- States are block-diagonal: $\varphi \leftrightarrow (\lambda_1 \rho_1, \ldots, \lambda_N \rho_N)$ with $\sum \lambda_i = 1$, $\rho_i$ states on $M_{d_i}$
- GNS: $\pi_\varphi \cong \bigoplus_i \mathrm{id}_{d_i}^{\oplus r_i}$ where $r_i = \mathrm{rank}(\rho_i)$ (for $\lambda_i > 0$)
- Bound: $\sum_i r_i^2 \leq m + 1$

This is a *mixed* Carathéodory-Barvinok-Pataki bound. It constrains both the ranks within blocks and the number of active blocks simultaneously. This appears to be new even in finite dimensions.

#### 5d. Infinite-dimensional case → Quantum information (Weis-Shirokov)

If $\mathcal{A} = B(\mathcal{H})$ for a separable Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}$:
- States on $B(\mathcal{H})$ are normal states = density operators
- For a rank-$r$ density operator ($r$ possibly infinite): commutant dimension is $r^2$ (for finite $r$)
- Under $m$ constraints (e.g., energy bounds $\mathrm{Tr}(H_j \rho) \leq E_j$): extreme points satisfy $r^2 \leq m+1$
- For $m = 2$: $r \leq 1$, so extreme points are pure states

This recovers **Weis-Shirokov Theorem 3** as a special case. But our framework also handles non-Type-I algebras, where the face structure is much richer.

### 6. Applications

#### 6a. Noncommutative polynomial optimization (NPA/state polynomial hierarchies)

The NPA hierarchy (Navascués-Pironio-Acín) and state polynomial optimization (Klep-Magron-Volčič) produce SDP relaxations where feasible points are truncated moment matrices. At convergence, these correspond to states on free *-algebras or universal C*-algebras subject to polynomial constraints.

**Application:** At any finite level of the hierarchy, the extremal solutions (which are what SDP solvers find) have GNS representations with bounded commutant dimension. This gives structural information about optimal solutions:
- The GNS Hilbert space decomposes as $\bigoplus_j \mathcal{H}_j^{\oplus n_j}$ with $\sum n_j^2 \leq m+1$
- For trace optimization: the optimal state factors through a finite-dimensional representation of bounded size

**Key references:**
- Klep, I., Magron, V., Volčič, J.: *State polynomials: positivity, optimization and nonlinear Bell inequalities*. Math. Program. (2023).
- Navascués, M., Pironio, S., Acín, A.: *A convergent hierarchy of semidefinite programs characterizing the set of quantum correlations*. New J. Phys. 10, 073013 (2008).

#### 6b. Quantum marginal problems

Given $k$ reduced density matrices (marginals) $\rho_1, \ldots, \rho_k$ of subsystems, the set of global states compatible with these marginals is an affinely constrained state space. Our theorem bounds the "complexity" (commutant dimension) of extremal compatible states.

#### 6c. Quantum channel optimization

Energy-constrained quantum channels: Weis-Shirokov applied their theorem to prove that extreme points of energy-constrained channel sets are "simple." Our framework extends this to channels between general operator algebras.

### 7. The Face Dimension Gap Phenomenon

**This is perhaps the most conceptually interesting part.** Different algebras have different "spectra" of possible face dimensions in their state spaces:

| Algebra $\mathcal{A}$ | Face dimensions in $S(\mathcal{A})$ | Gap after 0 |
|---|---|---|
| $C(X)$ (abelian) | $\{0, 1, 2, \ldots\}$ | None |
| $M_n(\mathbb{C})$ | $\{0, 3, 8, 15, \ldots, n^2-1\}$ = $\{d^2-1 : 1 \leq d \leq n\}$ | $\{1,2\}$ |
| $B(\mathcal{H})$ (inf-dim) | $\{0, 3, 8, 15, \ldots\} \cup \{\infty\}$ | $\{1,2\}$ |
| $M_2 \oplus M_2$ | $\{0, 3, 7\}$ | $\{1,2\}$ |
| Group C*-algebra $C^*(\Gamma)$ | Depends on $\hat{\Gamma}$ | Varies |

The gap determines how many constraints are "for free" — if all face dimensions jump from 0 to $\geq d$, then $\leq d$ constraints automatically force extreme points to be pure.

**Weis-Shirokov Corollary 7 (restated):** If $S(\mathcal{A})$ has no faces of dimension $1, 2, \ldots, \ell-1$ (other than $\{0\}$), then extreme points under $\leq \ell$ affine constraints are pure states (extreme points of $S(\mathcal{A})$ itself).

For $B(\mathcal{H})$: gap is $\{1,2\}$, so $\leq 2$ constraints ⟹ extreme points are pure. This is Weis-Shirokov's main quantum result.

For abelian algebras: no gap, so even 1 constraint can produce non-pure extreme points (convex combinations of point masses).

## Critical Mathematical Pitfalls to Avoid

### P1: Real vs Complex
The classical BP bound for *real* SDP is $r(r+1)/2 \leq m$. For *complex* (Hermitian) SDP it's $r^2 \leq m$. The commutant approach naturally gives the complex version. State clearly which setting you're in. When working with *-algebras over $\mathbb{C}$, the self-adjoint part of $M_r(\mathbb{C})$ has real dimension $r^2$.

### P2: The "+1" in $m+1$
Be precise about whether the trace/normalisation constraint $\varphi(1) = 1$ is counted among the $m$ constraints or not. Convention: the state space $S(\mathcal{A})$ already incorporates normalisation. The $m$ constraints are *additional* affine constraints on the state space. So the "+1" in $\sum n_j^2 \leq m + 1$ comes from the fact that $\dim(F) = \dim_{\mathbb{R}}(\pi'{}^{sa}) - 1$ (the "$-1$" is the normalisation within the face).

### P3: Finite vs infinite dimensional algebras
The Weis-Shirokov theorem works in full generality (no finite-dimensionality assumed). But the face-commutant correspondence needs care in the infinite-dimensional case:
- For C*-algebras: the face generated by $\varphi$ in $S(\mathcal{A})$ is affinely isomorphic to the *normal* state space of $\pi_\varphi(\mathcal{A})''$ (the von Neumann algebra generated). The commutant is $\pi_\varphi(\mathcal{A})'$.
- If $\dim_{\mathbb{R}}(\pi_\varphi(\mathcal{A})'^{sa}) < \infty$, then $\pi_\varphi(\mathcal{A})'$ is a finite-dimensional von Neumann algebra $\cong \bigoplus M_{n_j}$, and the face is finite-dimensional.
- If the face is infinite-dimensional, our bound says $\pi_\varphi(\mathcal{A})'^{sa}$ is infinite-dimensional, which means $\varphi$ cannot be extreme under finitely many constraints. This is the *contrapositive* that's useful.

### P4: Generalized affine maps
Weis-Shirokov work with generalized affine maps $f : K \to \mathbb{R} \cup \{+\infty\}$ (allowing $+\infty$). This is needed for unbounded observables (e.g., Hamiltonians on infinite-dimensional Hilbert spaces). If you restrict to bounded observables (= elements of the algebra), ordinary affine maps suffice and the argument simplifies. Handle both cases or clearly state your scope.

### P5: Exposed vs non-exposed faces
The face *generated by a point* (smallest face containing the point) is always well-defined. But it may not be *exposed* (i.e., not obtainable as $K \cap H$ for a supporting hyperplane $H$). The Weis-Shirokov theorem works with faces generated by points, which is the right notion. Don't confuse with exposed faces.

### P6: The quadratic module / Archimedean condition for NC optimization
When applying to noncommutative polynomial optimization, the Archimedean condition on the quadratic module $\mathcal{M}(S)$ is what ensures compactness of the "state space" and applicability of the theorem. State this connection precisely.

## Writing Style

- **Mathematical precision:** Every statement should have a proof, a precise reference, or be clearly marked as a remark/conjecture.
